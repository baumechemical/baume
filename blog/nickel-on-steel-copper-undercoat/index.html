TL;DR

  • Copper isn‚Äôt mandatory. With proper prep/activation and a Woods nickel strike, you can plate Nickel-on-steel directly.
  • Choose Cu undercoat for better throw in recesses, superior leveling before decorative Cr, or complex parts.
  • Cu downsides: extra step/cost and potential galvanic issues if Ni is too thin or porous.
  • Decorative corrosion resistance: go with Cu‚ÄìNi (duplex)‚ÄìCr.
Hardened steels (HRC > 40): perform hydrogen de-embrittlement bake after plating per spec.

When to use a copper undercoat

  • Leveling/porosity filling before Ni/Cr ‚Äî acid copper gives excellent leveling.
  • Complex parts/blind holes ‚Äî cyanide copper strike for improved throwing power (requires strict safety/waste control).
  • Solderability ‚Äî Cu improves wetting; follow with a thin Ni or other topcoat.
  • High decorative specs ‚Äî Cu 10‚Äì20 ¬µm + duplex Ni 20‚Äì35 ¬µm + micro-porous Cr.

When direct Ni works (no Cu)

  • General functional coatings ‚Äî Woods strike 2‚Äì5 min, then Watts/Sulfamate Ni.
  • Budget/time constraints ‚Äî fewer steps, less cross-contamination risk.
  • Clean fresh steel with robust alkaline clean + acid activation and controlled transfer/rinsing.

Two process routes

A) Direct nickel on steel

  1. Degrease/alkaline clean ‚Üí rinse
  2. Electro-clean (as suitable) ‚Üí DI rinse
  3. Acid activate (HCl/H‚ÇÇSO‚ÇÑ) ‚Üí quick rinse
  4. Woods nickel strike (NiCl‚ÇÇ + HCl, low pH) 2‚Äì5 min
  5. Watts/Sulfamate Ni to spec
  6. Optional: duplex Ni ‚Üí decorative Cr / ENP top

B) Copper undercoat route

  1. Degrease/alkaline ‚Üí rinse ‚Üí acid activate (avoid immersion film)
